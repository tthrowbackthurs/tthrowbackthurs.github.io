Historical Background

Right now, there are at least 2,060 satellites orbiting our planet. These satellites range in age from 45 years to only weeks, and serve a variety of purposes - from civil, commercial, government, military and more.

On October 4, 1957, the Soviet Union launched the first ever artificial satellite - Sputnik 1. The launch garnered attention from civilians around the world, as well as the United States government. Although aware of the progress the Soviets were making, the launch proved that the United States' biggest adversary was making technological advances.

Not to be outdone, the United States launched their first satellite, Explorer 1 on January 31, 1958, just under 4 months after the Soviets. Since this time, thousands of satellites have been launched - though many have passed their operational life and have either burned up in the Earth's atmosphere, or returning to Earth as wreckage.

As technology improves and becomes cheaper, there is an increased risk from the launch of so many satellites. From a risk of collision with space vehicles (including the ISS), to what could become the next battle ground, we're getting awfully crowded up there.
