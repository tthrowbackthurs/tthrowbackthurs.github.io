Historical Background

On the afternoon of August 26, 1883, the tremors and rumblings that had been silently rattling several Indonesian islands for years, reached a climax with the eruption of Krakatoa. As the black cloud of ash and smoke reached roughly 17 miles into the sky, the people in the region knew that this eruption was different than the small eruptions they had experienced previously.

When it was all said and done, the eruption, related earthquakes and tsunamis would devastate the region, killing more than 36,000 people and almost entirely obliterating the island of Krakatoa in the matter of just two days. The eruption is regarded as the loudest noise in history. The pressure wave and loud noise was responsible for rupturing the ear drums of sailors on ships 40 miles away. Even beyond this, the results were felt around the world – tsunamis affected ships in South Africa, ash permeated the atmosphere causing vibrant sunsets as far away as New York, and lowering the global climate by around two degrees Fahrenheit & disrupting patterns for five years.

Before the eruption of 1883, Krakatoa experienced at least three major events. Obviously Krakatoa is not the only major volcano in the world, but it illustrates how impactful volcanic eruptions can be, as well as how well volcanoes are recorded throughout history. Even when we don’t have recorded accounts of volcanoes, they tend to leave a mark on the environment, and geologists are able to identify historic eruptions worldwide.

This is good news for us and our dataset for this month as we explore volcanic eruptions worldwide. The data comes from the Smithsonian Institution Global Volcanism Program, and covers volcanoes and eruptions from the Holocene period (roughly the last 10,000 years).
